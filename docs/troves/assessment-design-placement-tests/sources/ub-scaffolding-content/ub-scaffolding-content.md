# Scaffolding Content

## Scaffolding Through Design

Scaffolding is an instructional practice where a teacher gradually removes guidance and support as students learn and become more competent. Support can be for content, processes, and learning strategies. This requires:

- **Initial assessment of students' prior knowledge**
- **Monitoring of growth** to determine which supports are needed and which can be removed
- As students grow, they begin more difficult challenges requiring new supports that eventually fade

The goals of scaffolding are to increase student proficiency and develop their skills as self-regulated learners.

### Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

ZPD is the difference between what students can do independently and what they can do with support. Teaching below the ZPD = reviewing what students already know (minimal learning). Teaching above the ZPD = frustration and failure (limited learning). Teaching within the ZPD allows students to use prior knowledge meaningfully while receiving guidance to eventually reach learning outcomes independently.

## Scaffolding Process

1. Determine what students already know (prior knowledge assessment)
2. Set a goal for learning (learning outcomes)
3. Plan instructional supports and how skills/strategies can be broken down
4. Implement lessons and monitor progress (formative assessment/feedback)
5. Fade support over time for students to become independently successful
6. Continue building on content, monitoring, and providing feedback

## Scaffolding Strategies

| Category | Strategies |
|---|---|
| **Unit and Lesson Planning** | Determine prior knowledge; chunk complex skills into smaller bites; divide instruction into mini-lessons with checkpoints; logically structure courses; provide additional supports, resources, references |
| **Instructional Practices** | Share lesson goals; build on prior knowledge; model skills and strategies; guided instruction and practice; multi-modal instruction; peer collaboration; examples and exemplars; provide steps and procedures |
| **Monitoring Learning** | Regular feedback and guidance; formative assessments; answer keys/self-checks; guide students to take ownership; adjust instruction based on assessment |
| **Learning Activities** | Guided practice; group work; collaborative writing; discussion boards; prompts and guiding questions; chunking large assignments; outlines, guided notes, graphic organizers; self-checks/reflections; formative assessments |

## Explicit Instruction Model (I Do, We Do, You Do)

- **I Do**: Instructor models and thinks aloud
- **We Do**: Students work together with instructor
- **You Do**: Students work independently

## Connection to Assessment

Scaffolding begins with assessing prior knowledge (diagnostic assessment) and uses formative assessment throughout to monitor progress and determine when to fade support. This creates a direct link between placement/diagnostic assessment and the educational scaffolding that follows.

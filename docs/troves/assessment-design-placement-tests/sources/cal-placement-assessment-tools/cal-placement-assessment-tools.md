# Types of Assessment Tools and Approaches for Placement

## Multiple-choice Tests

Typically consist of a series of statements or questions with a few answer choices. Easy to administer and score, commonly used to measure knowledge of grammar or vocabulary. More efficient for placement purposes but does not allow a comprehensive understanding of what students can do. Does not align well with proficiency-based approaches to teaching and learning.

## Performance-based Tasks

Students demonstrate what they can do in authentic, real-world situations. Can capture knowledge and abilities in different domains. More time-consuming to develop, administer, and score. May not be appropriate for programs with limited resources.

## Oral Interviews

Increasingly used in combination with other methods. Focus on assessing oral proficiency and overall communicative competence. May be informal (administered and scored in-house, designed to simultaneously gather background information) or formal (external administrators and raters, e.g., ACTFL Oral Proficiency Interviews). Time-consuming and may need supplementation by questionnaires and written tests.

## Self-assessments and Questionnaires

Ask students about backgrounds, experiences, interests, needs, and abilities. Research shows they can be extremely valuable for placement decisions when used as supplementary resources. Self-assessment allows students agency in placement while helping them reflect on their skills. NCSSFL-ACTFL Can-Do Statements can guide self-assessment and align with course expectations. Unreliable if used in isolation and students are not honest about their abilities. Particularly useful for placing students with heritage/community language experience or time abroad.

## Shortcut Proficiency Tests

Provide quick estimations of overall proficiency. Examples include C-tests (complete second half of every second word) and elicited imitation tasks (listen and repeat sentences). Efficient to administer and score, adaptable for multiple languages. Require specialized development processes and technical knowledge to create.

## Key Takeaways

- No single placement tool is sufficient; multiple measures are recommended
- Self-assessment supports learner agency and reflection
- Shortcut proficiency tests offer efficiency but limited depth
- Performance-based tasks offer depth but are resource-intensive
- The choice depends on local context, resources, and program type

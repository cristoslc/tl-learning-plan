# Effective Student Assessment and Placement: Challenges and Recommendations

**Authors**: D. Patrick Saxon & Edward A. Morante

## The Problem

About 42% of entering community college students are underprepared for college academic work (NCES). Certain student groups are overrepresented: 72% of African-American and 57% of Hispanic students at two-year colleges need developmental courses (CCA, 2013). Placement directly into college-level courses for underprepared students is controversial — allowing students the "right to fail" is likely destructive.

## Support for Mandatory Assessment and Placement

- Boylan (2002): Strongly advocates mandatory placement when quality courses and instruction are in place
- Bettinger & Long (2005): Students completing developmental math and writing were significantly more likely to persist
- Boylan, Bliss & Bonham (1997): Mandatory placement contributed to student success in developmental courses
- 13 states and 17 college systems had mandates requiring assessment and placement (Fulton, 2012)

## Challenges and Criticisms

### Lack of Prior Information and Preparation
Students often don't understand the importance of placement tests. They're not told about tests until after admission. Few colleges provide practice exams. Students report test content lacked connection to high school content.

### Inappropriate Uses of Test Scores
97% of two-year colleges used either COMPASS or ACCUPLACER. Tests commonly used in binary capacity (cut score = ready/not ready), ignoring standard error of measurement. No test gives an absolutely exact measure — scores should be considered in ranges.

### Failure to Include Other Measures
Test scores are a snapshot at a single point in time. Only about 7% of two-year colleges conduct any form of noncognitive assessment. Affective variables (motivation, self-regulation, assertiveness) explain ~41% of grade variance (Zientek et al., 2013). Multiple measures including high school GPA, background, and noncognitive factors should supplement test scores.

### Inadequate Advising Systems
Only 47% of advisors report adequate training. Only 37% feel adequately trained for special populations. Advising is ranked among lowest quality campus services (Astin, 1993).

### Predictive Validity Limitations
Placement tests are measures of achievement, not aptitude — designed to estimate current skill levels, not predict future performance. Low predictive validity for commonly used instruments (Hughes & Scott-Clayton, 2011). Demographic and affective variables explain more variance in success than test scores alone. Both advocates and critics agree: tests should not be the sole consideration.

## Recommendations

1. **Manage Transition**: Align skills with local high schools; practice and take placement tests in high school; provide feedback and career guidance
2. **Require Mandatory Assessment, Placement, and Advising**: Make these services mandatory, not optional
3. **Coordinate and Integrate Assessment Services**: Strong coordination with marketing, registration, orientation; eliminate late registration
4. **Modify Placement Tests for Diagnostic Use**: More diagnostic assessment instruments; North Carolina did this particularly for mathematics; additional questions serve dual placement and diagnostic purposes
5. **Strengthen Bridge Programs**: Summer programs that improve skill proficiencies and decrease need for developmental education
6. **Use Test Cut Score Ranges**: Instead of single cut scores, use ranges where other data inform placement decisions; align with SEM of the test
7. **Use Multiple Variables**: High school GPA (proxy for many performance and affective attributes), years out of school, courses taken and grades, motivation, maturity; student opinions with caution — recent graduates overestimate, long-absent students underestimate
8. **Maintain Inventories, Develop Student Attribute Profiles, Train Advisors**: Current inventory of courses and services; advisor training on profiles and options
9. **Evaluate Systematically**: Periodic content evaluation by curriculum/faculty experts; faculty rating system for placement accuracy

## Key Insight for Pedagogical Scaffolding

The authors emphasize that assessment should not be an event (take a test, get a score, get an up-or-down vote on courses) but rather "a coordinated process where students are informed about the test, advised on what it means to them, and offered assistance in practicing and preparing." This philosophy aligns directly with designing scaffolding systems that help learners determine their own best starting point.
